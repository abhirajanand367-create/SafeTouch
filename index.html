What Are Good Touch & Bad Touch?

Understanding the difference between safe and unsafe touches is a key life skill that protects children and builds confidence.

✅

Good Touch

Good touches are safe, caring, and make you feel comfortable and loved.

  • A warm hug from a parent with your permission
  • Holding hands with a friend during play
  • A doctor examining you with a parent present
  • A pat on the back for encouragement
  • A high-five to celebrate something
✓ Feels Good ✓ With Permission ✓ Makes Happy
🚫

Bad Touch

Bad touches make you feel scared, hurt, confused, or uncomfortable.

  • Any touch that makes you feel scared or uncomfortable
  • Touching private body parts
  • Touching done in secret or with gift promises
  • Being hit or physically hurt by someone
  • Any touch with threats like 'don't tell anyone'
✗ Feels Wrong ✗ Done in Secret ✗ Makes Scared
🔑 The Golden Rule: Your body belongs to YOU. No one has the right to touch you in a way that makes you feel uncomfortable. Your feelings are always valid.
⚖️ Rights

Your Body, Your Rights

1

Right to Say No

You can say No to any touch that makes you uncomfortable.

2

Right to Tell

It is NEVER your fault. Tell a trusted adult right away.

3

Right to Privacy

Your body is private. No one can touch private parts without a medical reason.

4

Feelings Matter

If something feels wrong, trust your instincts.

5

No Body Secrets

Safe adults do NOT ask for secrets about touches.

6

Never Your Fault

If touched inappropriately, it is NEVER your fault.

🗣️ Talk to Someone You Trust

Tell a parent, teacher, or counselor. If the first person doesn't help, tell someone else.

💡 Safety Guide

Staying Safe — Complete Guide

Detailed advice for children, adolescents, and parents on body safety and boundaries.

🧒 For Young Children (Ages 3–8) ▾

Teaching young children about body safety should be simple and never scary. Use correct names for body parts. Teach them the bathing suit rule: no one should touch areas covered by a bathing suit except a doctor with a parent present. Practice saying No loudly. Remind them that keeping secrets about bodies is never okay.

🧑 For Adolescents & Teens (Ages 9–17) ▾

Adolescents should understand that unwanted touching is never their fault, regardless of what they were wearing or where they were. Online safety is equally important: no one should ask you to share private photos. If an online contact makes you uncomfortable, block them and tell a trusted adult. Peer pressure can sometimes involve touching — it's okay to say no to friends too.

👨‍👩‍👧 For Parents & Caregivers ▾

Regular, calm conversations about body safety are more effective than one-time talks. Listen actively when your child talks about discomfort with any person, even a family member. In most cases of child abuse, the perpetrator is known to the child and family. If your child discloses abuse: stay calm, believe them, thank them for telling you, and seek professional help immediately.

🚩 Warning Signs to Watch For ▾

Warning signs include: sudden changes in behavior, nightmares, regression to younger behaviors, fear of specific people or places, unexplained physical symptoms. Adult grooming behaviors include: seeking one-on-one time with a child, giving gifts, encouraging secrecy, and isolating a child from family.

🛡️ Building a Safety Network ▾

Help your child identify at least 5 trusted adults they can go to if they feel unsafe. Teach children that if one adult doesn't help, tell another and keep telling until someone helps.

🌐 Online Safety & Digital Boundaries ▾

Body safety education must include online safety. No online friend should ask for photos or personal information. Establish clear family rules about screen time. If children receive unwanted images or messages, they should show a trusted adult immediately.
