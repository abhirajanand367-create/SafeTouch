What Are Good Touch & Bad Touch?

Understanding the difference between safe and unsafe touches is a key life skill that protects children and builds confidence.

✅

Good Touch

Good touches are safe, caring, and make you feel comfortable and loved. They respect your body and personal space.

  • A warm hug from a parent or family member with your permission
  • Holding hands with a friend during play
  • A doctor examining you with a parent or guardian present
  • A gentle pat on the back for encouragement
  • A high-five or fist bump to celebrate something
  • A hair wash or bath given by a trusted caregiver
✓ Feels Good ✓ Asked Permission ✓ Done in Public ✓ Makes You Happy
🚫

Bad Touch

Bad touches make you feel scared, hurt, confused, or uncomfortable. No one should touch you in a way that feels wrong — even someone you know.

  • Any touch that makes you feel scared or uncomfortable
  • Touching private body parts (covered by a swimsuit)
  • Touching done in secret or with promises of gifts
  • Being hit, punched, or physically hurt by someone
  • Touching that comes with threats like "don't tell anyone"
  • Any touch that feels wrong even if you can't explain why
✗ Feels Wrong ✗ Done in Secret ✗ No Permission ✗ Makes You Scared
🔑 The Golden Rule: Your body belongs to YOU. No one — not even a relative, teacher, or friend — has the right to touch you in a way that makes you feel uncomfortable. Your feelings are always valid. If something feels wrong, it probably is.
🩲 Privacy

Private Body Parts

Private parts are the areas covered by a swimsuit. No one should touch these areas except for medical reasons with a parent or guardian present.

🙋 Why We Call Them Private

Private parts are areas of your body that are special and belong only to you. They include the chest, bottom, and genitals — the areas covered by a swimsuit. Even on the beach or at a doctor's office, these areas deserve respect and privacy.

👨‍⚕️ When It's Okay for a Doctor

A doctor or nurse may need to check private areas during a health exam — but only with your parent or guardian in the room, with your understanding of why, and with your permission whenever possible. You can always ask questions.

⚖️ Your Rights

Your Body, Your Rights

Every person has the right to feel safe in their body. These are the fundamental rights you have.

1

The Right to Say No

You can say "No" to any touch that makes you feel uncomfortable — even from people you know and love.

2

The Right to Tell

If someone touches you in a wrong way, it is NEVER your fault. Tell a trusted adult right away and keep telling until someone helps.

3

The Right to Privacy

Your body is private. No one should look at or touch your private parts without a medical reason and a parent present.

4

Your Feelings Matter

If something feels wrong — even if you can't explain it — trust your instincts. Your feelings are real and important.

5

No Secrets About Bodies

Safe adults do NOT ask you to keep secrets about touches. Any "secret" about your body is a warning sign.

6

Never Your Fault

If someone touches you inappropriately, it is NEVER your fault. You are not to blame, no matter what anyone says.

🚨 What To Do

If Something Feels Wrong

🗣️ Talk to Someone You Trust

Tell a parent, teacher, school counselor, police officer, or another trusted adult. If the first person you tell doesn't help, tell someone else. Keep telling until someone listens and helps you.

📞 Know Emergency Numbers

Remember: You can always call emergency services (like 100 in India or 911 in the US). Child helplines are also available — they are free, confidential, and staffed by people who want to help you stay safe.

🆘

If you are in danger, call for help immediately

India: Child Helpline — 1098 (free, 24/7)  |  Police — 100  |  Women's Helpline — 1091. Tell a trusted adult if you or someone else is unsafe.

💡 Safety Tips

Staying Safe — A Complete Guide

Detailed advice for children, teenagers, and parents on body safety, boundaries, and what to do if something feels wrong.

🧒For Young Children (Ages 3–8) ▼

Teaching young children about body safety should be simple, age-appropriate, and never scary. Use correct names for body parts — this helps children communicate clearly if something happens. Teach them the "bathing suit rule": no one should touch the parts covered by a bathing suit except a doctor with a parent present.


Practice saying "No!" loudly and clearly. Role-play scenarios where they practice telling a trusted adult. Remind them that keeping secrets about bodies is never okay — there are no "body secrets." Emphasize that they will never get in trouble for telling the truth about a touch, even if the person said it was a secret.


Books, puppets, and simple games make these conversations easier and less intimidating for young children.

🧑For Older Children & Teens (Ages 9–17) ▼

As children grow, the conversations around body safety need to expand. Teens should understand that unwanted touching is never their fault, regardless of what they were wearing, where they were, or what they were doing. Consent is a two-way agreement — both people must freely agree.


Online safety is equally important: no one should ask you to share private photos, keep online friendships a secret, or meet up without telling a parent. If an online contact makes you uncomfortable, block them and tell a trusted adult immediately.


Peer pressure can sometimes involve touching. It's okay to say no to friends too. True friends respect your boundaries. If someone makes you feel like you "owe" them physical contact, that is a red flag.

👨‍👩‍👧For Parents & Caregivers ▼

Regular, calm conversations about body safety are more effective than one-time talks. Start early and revisit the topic as your child grows. Use everyday moments — bath time, doctor visits — as natural opportunities to reinforce body safety concepts.


Listen actively when your child talks about discomfort with any person or situation, even if that person is a family member, neighbor, or trusted community member. Statistics show that in most cases of child abuse, the perpetrator is known to the child and family.


Teach your child that their body belongs to them — including from family members. Forcing hugs or kisses, even from grandparents, can inadvertently teach children that they don't have control over their bodies. Instead, offer options: "Would you like to hug Grandma, wave, or give a high five?"


If your child discloses abuse: stay calm, believe them, thank them for telling you, and seek professional help immediately. Do not investigate on your own or confront the alleged abuser before consulting authorities.

🚩Warning Signs to Watch For ▼

Changes in a child's behavior can sometimes signal that something is wrong. Warning signs include: sudden changes in behavior or personality, nightmares or sleep disturbances, regression to younger behaviors (bedwetting, thumb-sucking), fear of specific people or places, unexplained physical symptoms, and reluctance to discuss what happened at certain places.


These signs don't always indicate abuse — they can be caused by many factors — but they warrant a gentle, supportive conversation and possibly professional guidance.


Adult grooming behaviors include: seeking one-on-one time with a child, giving gifts or special privileges, encouraging secrecy, gradually testing physical boundaries, and isolating a child from family and friends. Trust your instincts as a parent.

🛡️Building a Safety Network ▼

Help your child identify at least 5 trusted adults they can go to if they feel unsafe. This list might include a parent, grandparent, teacher, school counselor, or neighbor. Practice by asking: "If I'm not available and something scary happens, who would you tell?" Make sure these people know they are on your child's safety network.


Teach children that if one adult doesn't help or believe them, they should tell another adult and keep telling until someone helps. Persistence is important — children sometimes encounter disbelief and should know this is not their fault.


Memorizing emergency numbers and the family's home address (for younger children) is a practical safety skill. Role-play calling for help so children feel confident doing it in a real situation.

🌐Online Safety & Digital Boundaries ▼

In today's world, body safety education must include online safety. Explain to children that online relationships with strangers can carry risks, even if the person seems kind or friendly. No online friend should ask for photos, personal information, or secret meetings.


Establish clear family rules about screen time, which apps and platforms children can use, and the importance of telling parents about anyone who makes them uncomfortable online. Ensure privacy settings are appropriately set on all devices.


Remind children: anything posted online can be saved and shared. Even if they trust someone, sharing private images is never safe. If they ever receive unwanted images or messages, they should show a trusted adult immediately and not delete the messages (they may be needed as evidence).
